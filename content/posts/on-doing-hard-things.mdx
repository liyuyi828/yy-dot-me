title: "On doing hard things"
description: "A short reflection on why difficulty is often the point, not the cost."
date: "2026-04-10"
tags: ["self improvement", "philosophy"]

There&rsquo;s a category of advice I used to roll my eyes at: *do hard things*. It sounds like a slogan on a gym poster.

I&rsquo;ve come around to it, mostly because I&rsquo;ve noticed the pattern in my own life:

- The skills I&rsquo;m proud of came from work that was, at the time, somewhere between uncomfortable and miserable.
- The decisions I respect myself for were the ones I almost didn&rsquo;t make because they were inconvenient.
- The relationships that matter required conversations I would rather have skipped.

The shape of the pattern is: **the cost of the hard thing is usually paid up front; the reward is paid out, with interest, for years.**

Easy things have the opposite shape. The reward is immediate and the cost is paid quietly, in the background, in the form of a life that doesn&rsquo;t accumulate.

> &ldquo;The obstacle is the way.&rdquo;
> &mdash; Marcus Aurelius (and roughly half of the self-help section)

I don&rsquo;t think hard things are good *because* they&rsquo;re hard. The difficulty isn&rsquo;t magical. But difficulty is often a useful proxy for *importance* &mdash; the things that compound usually require something of you that the easy version doesn&rsquo;t.

So: when I find myself instinctively choosing the easier of two paths, I try to at least notice it. Sometimes the easy path is genuinely the right one. But if I can&rsquo;t articulate *why* it&rsquo;s the right one, the difficulty itself is signal.
